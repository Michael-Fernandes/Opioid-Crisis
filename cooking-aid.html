Cooking Aid

Get your hands dirty, not your phone.

Overview

I designed CookingAid to support cooks before, during and after a cooking experience.

The system uses a mobile and wearable application designed using a user-centered process.

To inform the design, I made decisions based on the findings from ethnographic research I previously conducted.

The end result allows users to find and review recipes, prepare a shopping list, and most importantly receive assistance while cooking a recipe.

Background and Research

A past exercise in ethnographic research gave me insight into the struggles and pains of home cooks. Inspiring me to create CookingAid.

The research I conducted previously used 4 semi-structured interviews and 3 contextual inquiries sessions to create insights into the behaviors of the home chef.

  • Depending on their cooking style, home cooks are either have either high or low context information needs.
  • While cooking a recipe, most home cooks struggle to remember a step(this can be either ingredient related or cooking action)
  • Home chefs participate in additional behaviors beyond cooking including socializing, recipe research and diary documentation.

Taking Action

Having the knowledge of pain points cooks faced during the cooking process compelled me to create a solution.

A technical solution seemed appropriate here since most cooks already use the internet to support their cooking habits.

I began my design process by thinking about the correct technology that could provide a solution for the design challenge.

Choosing the Correct Form

To choose a medium, I choose not to include technology that was ill suited for the kitchen. For example, a Voice Assistant might be hard to use in a noisy kichen and a phone screen is hard to use if your hands are covered with food.

A wearable solution ended being the best technology because it allowed multiple forms of interaction(touch, gesture and voice).

Basic Structure

After selecting the technology, I began structuring the app itself. I started by brainstorming activities that home cooks do when cooking. Next, I used the following stateflow to map the actions cooks carry out when cooking.

Paper Prototype

I choose to use a paper prototype to test the feasibility of using a smart-watch for cooking. To test for the actual act of cooking, I made prop cooking objects(made out of paper), to help simulate the act of cooking.

Wireframe Mockups

Usability tests done with the paper prototype found that found that the "wearable as a kitchen assistant" concept worked well. People easily and intuitively utilized the prototype to make a dish.

Using the original prototype as a starting point, I created additional features motivated by research. Previously I found that the cooks I studied actively search the web for the "correct" recipe(s), curate a shopping list and enjoy sharing and storing past recipes they cook.

To flesh out the app further, I embedded the features in a wireframe prototype.

High Fidelity

I used wireframes as a tool to talk about my design with cooks and fellow designers.

From those I showed the design with, I got feedback on the layout, features and general concept of the app.

With this feedback, I used my wireframe prototype as a blueprint to make a high fidelity interactable prototype. Again the prototype was adjusted through informal usability tests and show-and-tell sessions.

Final Thoughts

Through this process, I found, that the protyping technology for wearables is lacking. To truly test the effectiveness of this type of app, its necessary to test the same app idea in a real world context. I plan to do this in the future using the same mockup screens used for the high fidelity mockup and making a simple prototype in swift.
