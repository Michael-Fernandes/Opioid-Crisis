Uncertain Bus

Even with the help of mobile trip-planning apps, bus riders can struggle to make a decision of when to leave to catch a bus. Trip-planning apps use realtime bus arrival data to provide predictions of when of when a bus is most likely to come but, do not indicate that those predictions are uncertain. Uncertain Bus's interface communicates the concept of uncertainity to lay audiences, allowing people to make better decisions about every day events in their lives.

Major Finding:

Low-density Quantile Dot Plots (with 20-50 dots) and Cluminative Density Plots produce the best decisions made using realtime transit decisions.
This project further offers a methodology to evaluate decision making done using visualizations or statistics. Read our paper submitted to CHI 2018 to find out more about the research done around the design work discussed below.

My Role

My role with the Uncertain Bus project lasted ~1.5 years and resulted with a published paper in the confrence of Computer Human Interaction. While working on the project, I took on multiple responsibilities including front-end developer, data analyst, Design researcher and technical writer. The following focuses on how my role as UX designer led to the sucessful testing of 20 different display types.

Problem and Background

Interpreting predictions made by computers may introduce error into human decision making. A range of past work has shown that displaying uncertaininty can reduce these errors [ex1, ex2, ex3]. However, communicating the results of the decision in a way that is suitable for in the moment decisions and usable by a wide audience poses a challenge for designers. Up to this point, it has been unclear whether lay audiences can make decisions based on displays incpoperating uncertainity.

Uncertain Bus expands on past research done on establishing best pracitices for presenting uncertainity information from a machine learning system to bus riders. Using past research that established feasibility and design criteria for displaying uncertainity in realtime data, my team and I began by brainstorming possible visualizations and representations that we could add to the popular trip planning app OneBusAway.

Deciding When to Leave for a Bus is Hard!

The following scenario illustrates how uncertainity may affect bus riders.
  • Sara looks at OneBusAway to find her bus is predicted to arrive in 10 minutes. She decides to arrive at the stop in 9 minutes. Two things could happen:
  • A bus could come later : a bus may spend an abnormal time at a red light or a frazzled passenger might struggle to find the last quarter in his back pocket.
  • A bus could come earlier : a bus may catch consecutive green lights or ridership may be below normal.
  • In both scenarios, events outside of Sara's control affect her decision. Sara either spends extra time at a bus stop or misses the bus altogether.
Communicating the uncertainity that underlies the situation can benefit people's user experience both in catching the bus and using a realtime prediction data.

Framing Requirements

The intial goal of this project was to provide proof that people can use uncertainity information to make better decisions, specifically around realtime information. To achieve the goal, I set the following design goals:

  • Mantain the look and feel of current Trip Planning apps(OneBusAway was used as a reference).
  • Mantain a glanacable interface that can be used to make in the moment decisions.

To further understand the context of the design work I did, please refer to the paper submitted for peer review at CHI 2018. The following focuses on my role and contributions as a UX designer on the project.

Thinking Modularly

My team and I intially thought we would test 6 displays. I knew from this fact, my design would have to be interchangable for each display tested. Designing with a modular mindset ended up being key to the final result of the project. In the end, we evaluated over 20 different variations of displays.

For this project, my design process adapted in an agile way to fit the unique constraints of designing for a modular system. I began the design process by focusing on individual components before piecing the components together.

Since the basic parameters of the interface we're already set, I began by breaking down the larger interface into smaller components, I knew the final design would include. These pieces became the building blocks that I fit together, like a puzzle, to find the correct layout.

From the pieces mentioned previosuly, I created rapid iterations of the layout. I choose to use stand in information at this point in the design because the layout of information matters. In this context, what each informational piece means in the context of the larger interface determined the necessary form of the layout.

Connecting With Bus Riders

In order to mimic the same look, feel and most importantly, the information stored on OneBusAway within the Uncertain Bus interface. The challenge here came with integrating uncertainty displays into the OneBusAway system while maintaining glanceability. I solved this design problem by encoding the information found in OBA directly into the uncertainty visualization itself. Encoding information directly within uncertainty displays created a more cohesive and informationally-rich interface.

Motion for Convenience

In previous research, bus riders expressed need of making comparisons between multiple routes. To accommodate for this the interface incorporates a motion design that shifts the each bus timeline to the right, centering each visualization within the interface as it rises to the top. This animation allows bus riders to visually compare routes with relatively similar arrival times. I included this animation based on the previous findings that riders compare the arrival times of buses against each other to figure out what bus works best for them.

Somethings Just Don't Translate

During early usability tests, I found some of the information included in OBA did not make sense to people making decisions with UBUS. In particular, annotations that informed the user on how late or early a bus may arrive compared to its originally scheduled arrival time interfered with decision making. I found late-or-early annotations confused participants as to whether to pay attention to the uncertainty representation or the annotation. Since, in most cases, late annotations don't give an actual indication of the likelihood of a bus coming early or late, we choose to remove the annotations for testing purposes.

After Testing

I tested my design throughout the design process with guerilla usability tests(12+ people) and pilot tests of our online experiment (~90 people). After intial tests, 400+ people participated in our experiment. In short, our experiment asks participants to make decisions on when to leave for a bus based on a given scenario using a randomily assigned version of our interface as their decision-making tool. We used quantiative metrics(e.g. "point rewards" for catching the bus, time spent on each decision and minutes spent at the bus stop) collected throughout the experiment to determine how participants made decisions based off their assigned interface.

Final Design and Future Work

Our experiment shows that uncertainty displays can promote rational decision making however, more work must be done to see how users adapt to this type of interface in a real world situation.

To complete my role with the project, I completed designs based off the data collected from the experiment. I used the finding that Dot Plots allow for the best decision making around realtime transit data to create an interface that spatially accomodates for this particular design.
